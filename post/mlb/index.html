MLB Individual Game Player Performance

September 30, 2020

We predicted individual player performance on a game-by-game basis for purposes of constructing lineups for Daily Fantasy Sports (DFS).

Data

We used raw play-by-play data from Retrosheet.org, containing the results of every single at-bat since 2006. The data was processed to extract a range of information from each at-bat, and we checked aggregate team stats by year against BaseballReference.com to ensure the data was processed accurately.

High-level categories of the features we included in our data are: individual player skill, opposing pitcher skill, park factors, lineup position, and skill of teammates close to player in the lineup. Factors that we hypothesize would also impact results but we did not have time to include are: opposing bullpen skill, opposing team defense, weather forecast (outdoor stadiums only), pitcher style vs. batter preference (e.g. curveball pitcher vs. fastball hitter), and handling for rookies with limited data.

MLB DFS points is inherently random, with $0$ points being the most frequent result, even for Mike Trout, the best player in baseball:

Although the data is discrete, it roughly fits an exponential distribution. The fit lines pictured above are exponential with means of 8.1 for the league and 14.2 for Mike Trout.

Benchmarking

Because of the inherit randomness of our data, it was important for us to have benchmarks to evaluate the effectiveness of our model. We found a paper from Operations Research Professors at MIT where they were able to make $15,000 over 11 weeks playing NHL daily fantasy sports. These professors used predictions from the website Rotogrinders and constructed rosters using linear programming. They mentioned that they also preformed a hypothetical study on MLB daily fantasy sports that appeared to be profitable. Therefore, we used Rotogrinder’s predictions from 2017-2019 as our benchmark.

$$ \begin{array}{matrix_1} Year & R^2 & MAE & RMSE \\\
2017 & 2.39\% & 7.38 & 9.97 \\\
2018 & 2.73\% & 7.24 & 9.51 \\\
2019 & 3.03\% & 7.65 & 10.10 \\\
Total & 2.66\% & 7.42 & 9.87 \\\
\end{array} $$

Any model with metrics close to these would likely have value for actual DFS competitions. We also wanted to know what the theoretical best possible model would for this data distribution. We created a hypothetical thought experiment where we assumed career average points by player represented the distribution of means of exponential random variables representing individual game player points. Our prediction is set equal to the mean, which represents the best estimate of an exponential random variable, and our results were the resulting simulated values of these exponential random variables. We verified that the simulated data fit closely to the actual distribution of points.

Theoretical Best Possible Results:

$$ \begin{array}{matrix_2} R^2 & MAE & RMSE \\\
7.2\% & 5.65 & 8.00 \end{array} $$

Models

We averaged our stats based on the last 100, 250, 500, 1,000, and 2,000 at-bats prior to the game being predicted. We found the last 1,000 at-bats to be the most predictive, and included the ratio of points for the other time periods relative to the last 1,000 in the final data. We tried a wide array of machine-learning algorithms and various different architectures for dense neural nets. I also tried a 1D convolutional neural net on the raw data, using the last 500 at-bats (without averaging), thinking that the neural net would be able to determine a more optimal front-weighted averaging for combining the data. Our best results ended up coming from a gradient boosting regression model.

Results and Next Steps

Our Best Model:

$$ \begin{array}{matrix_2} Year & R^2 & MAE & RMSE \\\
2017 & 2.63\% & 7.53 & 9.89 \\\
2018 & 3.05\% & 7.32 & 9.52 \\\
2019 & 3.26\% & 7.70 & 10.09 \\\
Total & 2.99\% & 7.51 & 9.83 \\\
\end{array} $$

We were able to acheive a 12% improvement over our benchmark, and captured 42% of the theoretical maximum possible variance that can be explained. We believe further improvements can be made by incorporating the data not included as described above.

Once we are done improving the model, the next step is to construct 150 hypothetical lineups per day, designed to maximize expected value and variance of individual rosters, and minimize correlation amongst rosters. This will provide the best oppurtunity to take advantage of the top heavy payout structure used for these competitions. Results from historical competitions can be used to construct hypothetical profit and loss.
